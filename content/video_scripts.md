## Script 1: Meeting to Summary

**Style Prompt:** Speak in an energetic, confident, conversational tone like a knowledgeable friend sharing a productivity hack. Vary pace for emphasis. Sound genuinely excited about the solution.

---

**HOOK (10 sec)**

What if your meeting notes wrote themselves and actually captured what mattered?

**CONTEXT (30 sec)**

Here's the thing. Most people leave meetings with scattered notes, or worse, no notes at all. The information starts fading within hours. [sigh] We've all been there. Back-to-back meetings, trying to remember who said what, what we actually decided.

But with this one skill, you'll turn any meeting transcript into a structured, actionable summary before you've even left the room. And I mean sixty seconds. Not kidding.

**DEMO (3 min)**

So here's what a typical meeting transcript looks like. It's messy. It's long. People talking over each other, tangents about someone's weekend, that one person who always goes off-topic. [laughing] You know who I'm talking about.

Now, here's the magic prompt. Ready?

"Summarize this meeting with key decisions, action items with owners, open questions, and next steps."

That's it. Paste your transcript, add that prompt, and watch what happens.

[uhm] Now look at this output. Clean sections. Decisions clearly stated. Action items with actual names attached, not just "someone should follow up." Open questions flagged so nothing falls through the cracks.

But here's where it gets even better. You can refine it. Try this: "Add deadlines mentioned and highlight any risks discussed."

Boom. Now you've got a document that's better than anything you'd write manually. And it took, what, thirty seconds?

[whispering] Here's a secret most people don't realize. You can adapt this for different audiences instantly.

Same transcript, new prompt: "Create a two-sentence version for Slack."

Done. Executive summary for your boss, detailed version for the team, Slack update for stakeholders. Same meeting, three formats, under two minutes total.

The people who use this consistently? They become known as the person who always has their act together. The one who sends the clear follow-up. [sarcasm] Meanwhile, everyone else is still trying to decipher their handwritten notes three days later.

**SUMMARY (20 sec)**

Meeting to Summary is your first step toward what I call Meeting to Execution. Start with summaries, then graduate to having AI draft the actual deliverables discussed. The meeting ends, the work begins automatically.

**CTA (10 sec)**

Try this with your next meeting transcript. Just that one prompt. Next week, we go beyond summaries to actual outputs. See you then.

Old approach? Take notes, schedule time to write the proposal, probably take three days. [sarcasm] Maybe a week if we're being honest.

Now look at what comes back. It's not perfect, it never is on the first pass, but it's eighty percent there. It captured the client's stated goals. It referenced specific timelines they mentioned. It even flagged their budget concerns.

This is your first draft. In thirty seconds. Not three days. Thirty seconds.

[uhm] Now here's where your human judgment comes in. You review it. You adjust the tone. You add your expertise. But you're editing, not creating from scratch. Massive difference.

Let me show you another variation. Strategy session about a new product feature. The prompt shifts: "Based on this discussion, create a product requirements document with user stories, acceptance criteria, and technical considerations mentioned."

[whispering] Same principle, different output. The meeting generates its own artifacts.

Here's the mindset shift that matters. Stop thinking of meetings as time spent talking. Start thinking of them as content creation sessions. Every meeting is a draft waiting to happen.

The best part? Your clients and colleagues think you're incredibly fast. [laughing] You are. You're just not doing it the old way anymore.

**SUMMARY (20 sec)**

Meeting to Execution means the discussion becomes the first draft. You're not starting from blank pages. You're refining what already exists. That's the cyborg advantage.

**CTA (10 sec)**

**DEMO (3 min)**

Now I'm getting the nuance. Not just what is, but what's contested. That's where the interesting insights live.

Third prompt: "If I were entering this market as a new player, what would I need to understand that isn't obvious from surface-level research? What do insiders know that outsiders miss?"

[whispering] This is gold. You're asking for the hidden knowledge. The stuff that takes years to learn through experience.

Finally: "Create a reading list. What are the five most important articles, reports, or resources someone should read to go deeper? Explain why each one matters."

Now you have a curated path forward. Not random googling. Targeted depth.

[laughing] In about fifteen minutes, you've gone from zero to informed enough to have a real conversation. Maybe not an expert, but dangerous enough to ask the right questions.

**SUMMARY (20 sec)**

Deep Dive Research is about structured curiosity. The right prompts in the right sequence take you from ignorance to insight faster than you thought possible. It's not about knowing everything. It's about knowing enough.

**CTA (10 sec)**

---

The same message, said differently, lands completely differently. Are you matching your tone to your audience?

[sigh] We all know this intuitively, but executing it? Switching between formal and casual, technical and accessible, urgent and calm? That takes effort. Unless you have help.

**DEMO (3 min)**

Let me show you tone matching in action. I've got an announcement about a product delay. Not fun news. Let's see how the same core message adapts.

First, internal team: "Rewrite this announcement for our internal engineering team. Be direct, acknowledge the challenge, focus on the path forward. Technical details are appropriate."

[uhm] Look at that. Straightforward. No spin. Acknowledges the situation, explains the cause, outlines next steps. Engineers appreciate directness.

Same news, different audience: "Now rewrite this for our executive leadership. Focus on business impact, mitigation steps, and timeline. Keep it brief and action-oriented."

Different emphasis. Less technical detail. More focus on outcomes and decisions needed. That's what executives need.

One more: "Rewrite this for our customer-facing support team. They'll be getting questions. Help them understand the situation and give them language to use with customers. Empathetic but confident."

[whispering] See how different that is? Support teams need to feel informed and equipped. The tone is reassuring because they need to reassure others.

Here's the mindset shift. Every piece of communication is actually multiple pieces of communication. Same core message, adapted for who's receiving it.

[laughing] I've used this for everything. Difficult feedback that needed to land gently. Exciting news that needed to sound professional. Technical explanations that needed to make sense to non-technical people.

The skill isn't writing different messages. It's knowing how to transform one message into many.

**SUMMARY (20 sec)**

Tone matching is empathy made practical. You're not changing what you say, you're changing how you say it for who's hearing it. That's the difference between communication and connection.

**CTA (10 sec)**

[laughing] These are your robust moves. The things worth doing no matter what. This is strategic clarity.

## Script 12: Decision Making

**Style Prompt:** Speak with thoughtful confidence, like a trusted advisor helping someone through a tough choice. Calm and wise. Create trust around structured thinking.

---

**HOOK (10 sec)**

Stuck on a decision? There's a better way than agonizing.

**CONTEXT (30 sec)**

[sigh] Big decisions are hard. Not because we lack intelligence. Because we lack structure. Our brains get overwhelmed by complexity, emotion, and uncertainty. We go in circles. We second-guess. We delay.

Structured decision making isn't about removing human judgment. It's about creating conditions where human judgment works better.

**DEMO (3 min)**

Let me show you decision support in action. Say I'm deciding whether to leave my job for a startup opportunity. Classic difficult decision. High stakes. High uncertainty.

First, we clarify: "Help me think through this career decision. First, let's identify what I actually need to decide. What's the core choice here, and what related questions do I need to answer?"

[uhm] See, already this is useful. The core choice is clear, but there are related questions. Timeline. Negotiation possibilities. Risk tolerance. We're mapping the decision space.

Now we structure: "What are the key factors I should weigh in this decision? Help me create a framework for evaluation. What matters most?"

Financial security. Career growth. Learning opportunity. Work-life balance. Mission alignment. Risk tolerance. The factors emerge. Now I can think about each one explicitly instead of them swirling in my head.

[whispering] Here's the prompt that changes everything: "Play devil's advocate for each option. What's the strongest case against taking the startup role? What's the strongest case against staying in my current position?"

This is crucial. We naturally favor one option and dismiss concerns about it. Forcing the counter-argument surfaces what we're avoiding.

Now we test: "What additional information would most change my thinking? What do I not know that I should try to learn before deciding?"

[laughing] Maybe I should talk to people who've made similar transitions. Maybe I should negotiate better terms. The decision might need more input before it needs more thinking.

Finally: "If I were advising a friend in exactly this situation, what would I tell them? Help me step outside my own emotional attachment."

Distance creates clarity. The advice we'd give others is often wiser than the choices we make for ourselves.

**SUMMARY (20 sec)**

Decision making isn't about finding the right answer. It's about thinking through the choice completely enough that you can commit with confidence. Structure enables clarity. Clarity enables action.

**CTA (10 sec)**

Take a decision you've been avoiding. Run it through this process. You might not find certainty, but you'll find clarity. And that's enough.

## Production Notes

### Voice Selection
- Recommended: **Kore** (Female) for warm, approachable delivery
- Alternative: **Charon** (Male) for authoritative presence

### Inline Tags Used
- `[sigh]` - Relatable frustration moments
- `[laughing]` - Light moments and self-aware humor
- `[uhm]` - Natural thinking pauses
- `[whispering]` - Revealing secrets/insights
- `[sarcasm]` - Self-deprecating or knowing humor

### Per-Script Style Prompts
Each script includes a customized style prompt at the top. These should be passed to the Gemini TTS API as the prompt parameter to set the overall emotional tone.

### Technical Specs
- Target duration: ~5 minutes per script
- Output format: MP3 or WAV
- Sample rate: 24000 Hz
- Language code: en-US

### Content Chunking
For streaming, scripts can be chunked at section breaks (HOOK, CONTEXT, DEMO, SUMMARY, CTA). The style prompt only needs to be passed with the first chunk.
